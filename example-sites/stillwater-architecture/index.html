01 · Practice

We design for the long weather. A house in Tasmania must hold warmth for eight months, hold wind for three, and hold a family for forty years. Anything else is just decoration.

Stillwater was founded by Mei Takashima and Rory Fitzwilliam in 2014, after each had spent a decade practising interstate. We design residential projects only — new homes on coastal and rural sites, additions to heritage fabric in Hobart, and long-view masterplans for working farms across the Midlands.

The four of us share a studio above Salamanca Place, a library of materials we have tested on our own buildings, and a belief that a good house should be easier to live in, not harder, than the one it replaced.

04 · Approach

Three things we measure every project against.

i · Climate

The building runs itself.

Every plan is tested for passive solar gain and loss across the twelve months of the Hobart year. Eaves are set by simulation, not by eye. If a house needs a heater to be warm in July, we haven't finished drawing it.

ii · Craft

Local hands, local timber.

We specify Tasmanian-grown hardwoods for every project that can accept them, and work with a core group of Tasmanian builders whose sites we visit fortnightly. A good detail survives its builder's patience; we don't draw ones that won't.

iii · Context

The site was already working.

Before we draw the house we walk the block for a season. Light, wind, drainage, neighbours, the single tree you shouldn't remove. A house that ignores its site ages badly. A house that listens — quietly, carefully — tends to improve with time.

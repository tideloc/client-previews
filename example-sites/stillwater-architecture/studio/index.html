Est. 2014

A small studio, very deliberately.

Stillwater is a four-person residential practice working out of a single room above Salamanca Place. We take five new projects a year. That number has not changed since 2017 and will not.

The studio, winter · Nipaluna / Hobart · Photo by Adam Gibson

On the practice

We wanted a studio small enough that the partners could run every project from first walk to last walk-through, and big enough to do careful work with good builders over long timelines. Four people, it turns out, is that number.

Mei and Rory had each worked in large practices — in Sydney and Melbourne respectively — before founding Stillwater. What they wanted to build here was a working life that had the capacity for long design and short, direct conversations. No juniors running sites alone. No drawings reviewed by people who had not walked the land.

Today we work almost exclusively for direct private clients — returning Tasmanians, tree-changers, second-home buyers with long horizons. Our projects range from $1.5M custom homes to $6M rural masterplans. Most take between eighteen months and three years from first meeting to move-in.

Principal architect, co-founder

Mei Takashima

B.Arch (Hons), M.Arch, RAIA, Reg. TAS 4881 / NSW 11492

Mei founded Stillwater with Rory in 2014 after eight years with Stutchbury and Pape in Sydney and two years at Candalepas Associates. She leads the rural work — masterplanning, shearing sheds, the long slow projects that measure themselves in trees planted rather than rooms built.

She teaches design studio one semester a year at the University of Tasmania and sits on the Royal Hobart Hospital public art advisory committee. She does most of her best thinking walking the kunanyi track at five in the morning.

Principal architect, co-founder

Rory Fitzwilliam

B.Des (UTas), M.Arch (RMIT), RAIA, Reg. TAS 5107

Rory runs the coastal and urban side of the practice — the additions, the boat-houses, the one-year jobs. He joined Six Degrees in Melbourne at 24, returned to Hobart in 2012, and co-founded Stillwater two years later. He cares very deeply about doorways.

He spent 2019 on a Byera Hadley travel scholarship studying adaptive reuse of vernacular stone structures in the Outer Hebrides and north Iceland. That research still shapes how we treat heritage fabric in the Midlands projects.

Lenny is responsible for most of our technical resolution and all of our passive-thermal modelling. If a house keeps you warm without trying, there is a good chance Lenny drew the detail that made it so. He joined the studio in 2019.

Our approach

How a project unfolds.

We work in five loose stages, each measured in weeks rather than days. Every stage closes with a working session on site — we do not produce drawings we have not walked through in person.

  1. 01

    Site

    Four walks across a season. Photographs, levels, soil, wind, trees, neighbours, access. One afternoon with the client at the kitchen table listing what matters.

  2. 02

    Brief

    Drafted by the partners over two weeks. Budget, gross floor area, room count, material palette direction, lifecycle assumptions. Signed before any sketching.

  3. 03

    Design

    8-14 weeks of concept through developed design. Monthly client meetings, fortnightly model updates, weekly internal reviews. Two major iterations, rarely three.

  4. 04

    Documentation

    12-16 weeks to construction-tender documentation. Fully resolved — every junction, every material, every fixing, every tree retained.

  5. 05

    Build

    14-24 months on site depending on scale. Fortnightly visits by the project partner. Snag walk at month 12, at lockup, at practical completion.

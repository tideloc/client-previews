Since 2016

A small roastery on
the NSW South Coast.

We didn't plan to open a roastery. We wanted to open a cafe that served coffee we liked. Turns out no one was roasting it the way we wanted, so we learned how. Ten years later we're still small, still in Thirroul, still roasting three times a week.

The story

Start with a cafe.
End up a roastery.

Elena opened the first Tidal cart at the Thirroul Seaside & Arts Festival in March 2016 — a borrowed single-group La Marzocco, a pop-up tent, and forty kilos of borrowed beans from a Sydney roaster. By the end of the weekend they'd sold out and Elena had a spreadsheet full of regulars asking when the permanent shop opened.

It took two years. In late 2018 we took over a narrow shopfront on Lawrence Hargrave Drive, between the bakery and the old bookshop. For the first eighteen months we bought beans from three different Sydney roasters, trying to find coffee that tasted the way we remembered it tasting in cafes overseas. None of them quite did.

In 2019 we rented a 1kg sample roaster and started teaching ourselves. Marcus came on that same year — he'd been roasting in Melbourne for five years and was looking for somewhere smaller. We commissioned the 12kg Probat eighteen months later. Every bean we sell now comes off that machine.

We've stayed small on purpose. One cafe. Three roast days. No fleet of trucks, no second site on the Gong. If we're ever tempted, Marcus reminds us we already have enough work.

How we source

Direct Trade,
not Fair Trade.

Fair Trade pays farmers a minimum — it's a floor. Direct Trade starts from a different question: what is this coffee actually worth, and who gets paid what share of it?

We buy through three importer partners we've worked with for seven years — Melbourne Coffee Merchants, Cofinet, and Caravela. They give us farm-level transparency: we know the name of the farm, the name of the producer, what they were paid, and (usually) how that compares to the C-market price that week.

Where we can, we visit. Elena's been to Finca La Palma in Tolima three times. Marcus spent a month in Rwanda in 2022 cupping with three co-operatives. It's not a marketing line; it's just how you build trust with people you're asking to farm at a standard most farmers aren't asked to hit.

We pay, on average, 68% above the C-market benchmark for the green coffee we buy. It's not a badge we shout about. It's just the cost of roasting coffee we want to drink.

The team

Two of us, mostly.

Founder & Director

Elena Marshall

Elena opened the first Tidal cart at the Thirroul Seaside & Arts Festival in 2016 with a single-group La Marzocco on loan. She'd spent the previous eight years running the bar at a small roaster in Fitzroy. She still sources every green bean, writes the subscription notes, and answers every customer email — usually before 7am.

Head Roaster

Marcus Okafor

Marcus came on in 2019 after five years roasting in Melbourne — including a stint at a farm-direct outfit in Preston. He runs the 12kg Probat three mornings a week, cups every sample lot before it goes on the menu, and has a near-religious attitude to development time ratios. A former chemical engineer, which somehow tracks.

Plus Tess, Jack, and Nina behind the bar most mornings.

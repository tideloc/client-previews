The story

Two homebrewers.
One leaky truss shed.

Ember & Grain started in 2017 in a 1957-built shadehouse on Federal Drive in the Byron hinterland. It's still there. We're still here. So are four out of five of the original beers.

Chapter 01 · 2014

A shed in Paddington.

Tom brewed his first batch in the garage of a share house in Paddington, Brisbane, on a 20L stove-top kit a housemate had found at the side of the road. The lager was terrible. The second one was less terrible. The third he entered into the Queensland Amateur Brewing Championships and took third in his category. By the time the certificate arrived in the post he'd bought a 50L system and quit his day job's overtime.

Chapter 02 · 2016

The move north.

Harriet finished her microbiology honours at Sydney Uni and moved up to Mullumbimby with a shared car, a Kombi starter culture, and a certainty that she'd either open a sourdough bakery or do something with mixed-culture fermentation. She and Tom met on a Saturday market day at Mullum Showgrounds; by Wednesday they'd agreed to go in on a brewhouse together. Neither of them had any money. Tom rang his dad.

Chapter 03 · 2017

The old truss shed.

The shed on Federal Drive was built in 1957 as a shadehouse for an orchid nursery, then spent thirty years as a truss manufacturer, then five years empty. It had the concrete slab we needed, three-phase power, an artesian bore, and a roof that leaked in four spots. We signed the lease on a Friday in August and brewed the first batch of Slow Road on the Sunday of the following weekend. The taproom opened eight weeks later, when we put out a chalkboard on the verge and sold out by 2pm.

Chapter 04 · 2022

Twelve taps, same shed.

We've grown from 4 taps to 12, from one brewhouse to two (a 200L brew deck out the back for small experimental stuff), and from two people to seven. We still brew every beer ourselves, still pour them across the same twelve-metre bar, and still do the accounts on Sundays over a pot of Federal Dark. We made 140,000 litres last year. We'd like to keep it roughly that size forever.

The brewers

Two people who still do all the brewing.

Tom Wren

Co-founder · head brewer

Tom spent twelve years as a civil engineer in Brisbane before giving it up in 2016 to finish his Brewing Diploma at Ballarat. Obsessed with lager, oats, and the quiet kind of complexity. Makes most of the recipe decisions. Plays a battered Telecaster in the taproom on Thursdays if you ask nicely.

Harriet O'Dell

Co-founder · fermentation lead

Harriet runs the barrel program, the mixed-culture cellar and the yeast lab upstairs. Trained as a microbiologist at Sydney Uni, kept a sourdough starter alive through three share-house moves, still has it. Responsible for every funky, sour, or foeder-aged thing on the board.

How we try to do less harm

Doing small, carefully.

We'll never be a huge brewery and we don't want to be. Staying small lets us make choices a bigger outfit can't — re-using spent grain on the farm next door, distributing ourselves in one truck, and brewing seven days a fortnight instead of twenty-one.

None of this is a marketing programme, it's just how we do it. But since you asked, here's what that looks like:

  • 01

    100% spent grain returned to land

    Every kilo of spent grain goes to a single dairy herd 4km up the road. In return we get milk in the coffee and a signed grazier's receipt.

  • 02

    Single-truck distribution

    We stock 45 indie shops inside a 60km radius. One Troopcarrier, one day a week. No interstate freight, no fridged pallets in Queensland.

  • 03

    Glass over aluminium where we can

    Growler program runs six days a week. $5 refills, no minimum. We sell more draft than packaged beer every year and we think that's the right way around.

  • 04

    100% renewable electricity

    32kW of solar on the truss roof, backed by a 60kWh battery that we top up off-peak from Enova Community Energy.

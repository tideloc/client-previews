// About the studio

A tin shed.
A coal fire.
Ten years in.

The Forge Works started in 2014 when Tom Halloran and Sara Chen took over a disused fencing-contractor's shed on the back of a 2-acre block in Braidwood. We've been here since.

Tom trained in Yorkshire under Peter Dyson and spent seven years there before moving back to Australia in 2010. Sara came through RMIT's sculpture program and cut her teeth on architect-led projects in Melbourne and country Victoria. They met on a job in Castlemaine in 2017 and pooled the two practices into one shed shortly after.

The place works like this: Tom runs the heritage commissions and the weekend classes. Sara runs the architectural work, the CAD, and all the drawings that go out to builders. Most projects pass through both of us. We make everything here — no production is outsourced. If it has our name on it, one of us forged it.

We don't have a big shopfront. We don't have a catalogue that turns over each season. Work takes the time it takes. The shed is cold in winter, the forge runs hot, and every three or four months someone drives out from Canberra or Sydney with an impossible brief and a photograph of a doorknob their grandmother once loved. We mostly say yes.

// The two makers

Tom & Sara.

01 · Hand forge + coal

Tom Halloran

Heritage blacksmith

Tom trained under Peter Dyson in the North Riding from 1998. He brings the traditional disciplines — forge-welding, hand-drawing, pattern replication — and a pretty unkind view of any shortcut involving electricity. He runs the heritage commissions and teaches the weekend classes.

Trained Yorkshire, 1998. Fourth-generation smith. · 26 years at the anvil

02 · Forge + fabrication + CAD

Sara Chen

Architectural metalworker

Sara came through the RMIT School of Art in 2011 and spent six years on architect-led projects in Melbourne before moving the operation to Braidwood with Tom in 2018. She works between the forge and the CAD station — the contemporary projects, the architectural liaison, the big-scale sculptural work.

Trained RMIT 2011. Forged, fabricated, drawn. · 13 years at the anvil

// The equipment

Tools with history.

Four of the important ones
01

1922 Alldays & Onions Blower

Hand-cranked · Sheffield · Restored 2015

Salvaged from a disused shearing shed near Goulburn. Needed new leather vanes, a replacement crank handle from a lathe-turn mate in Tallaganda, and a lot of patience. She's been in daily use since 2016.

02

Primary coal forge

600mm × 450mm hearth · welsh steam coal

Brick-built by Tom over six weeks in 2014, banded with mild-steel strap and lined with refractory cement. Fed by the Alldays blower. Runs Welsh anthracite — the only kind worth the money.

03

Peter Wright 250kg anvil

Dudley · c.1890 · rebound 89%

The main anvil. Cast-iron body with a tool-steel face, Dudley-stamped. Rings like a church bell. The face has been dressed once in a hundred-odd years — by us, gently, in 2014.

04

Sara's MIG rig + CAD station

Kemppi Minarc · Fusion 360

For the architectural work — where drawings, CNC and MIG live alongside the hammer. Sara runs the whole digital side of the shop from here. The forge will always win a debate, but the drawings have to be right first.
